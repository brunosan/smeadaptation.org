1 Dependence

In this step, think about how the company depends on the key sectors or provides products and services in these areas. Is it a large consumer of electricity? Does it depend upon water as an input in your products? Would a temporary blockage of road transport of its products and inputs create serious financial hardship?

Next, consider in which sectors the SME provides products or services. This may be an easier process if the SME's core business is growing or processing food, energy production or healthcare provision. For other companies that repair or build machinery, consider how its work contributes to the larger functioning of the sector.

2 Risks


Direct Data

For your priority sectors, first, look for any direct data on the current and projected conditions in this sector. The scale of measurement will be dependent on your business operations. Your operational scale may be at the community, state, regional or national level. Data will exist for some scales and be nonexistent or incomplete at others. Different strategies will need to be undertaken to account for this difference.

Local data can come from local trade associations and academic institutions/universities.
State and regional information can come from government databases and academic institutions/universities.
National data can come from government databases and academic institutions/universities

View this Sector Data excel sheet for state and national data for these sectors. In the SME Workbook, select whether the sector is Declining, Stable or Improving based on this research.

Global forces data

When there is a lack of direct data for your target sector, or, additional research is desired, look at global forces that impact the sector. This will require more research and engagement with the topic. Almost all trends in global forces, such as population change, precipitation variation and increases in natural disasters can impact the target sector. The leadership of the SME or an expert consultant will need to use his/her best judgement, based on knowledge of their own operations, on how these global forces affect priority sectors. For instance, a farmer that relies primarily on precipitation (instead of irrigation) will need to examine projections in precipitation change. A manufacturer dependent on a steady supply of electricity, may look at population and temperature trends to anticipate future increases in energy demand. For known data on these indirect data, see this Global Forces excel sheet.

In the SME Workbook, determine whether global forces are causing negative, neutral or positive trends in the priority sectors and tally the combined effects. Compare the assessment of the direct data with global forces and determine the final condition.

5 Strategy

Several strategies exist according to the vulnerability or opportunity present in each sector. Four key strategies include:

  • Investments in services or products - the SME can seek to expand in its current market by identifying specific services and products that can help communities adapt. Much of this will require new financial investments.

  • Change in internal practices - creating more efficient processes within the business that save energy, water and other resources. Some processes may require little capital investment while others may require installation of new machinery and technology.

  • Collaboration - reaching out to NGOs, other businesses and the government to develop solutions.

  • Gather information - there still may be no apparent path forward. New technologies and approaches may be needed. Check back with smeadaptation.org for solutions.

Depending on the investment environment and government engagement, one or all of these strategies might be pursued.
